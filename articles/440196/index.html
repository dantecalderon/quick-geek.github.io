Secrets of consciousness and mathematics

In ancient Egypt, mathematics did not use evidence. All their statements were only empirically substantiated. But nevertheless, the pyramids were standing, and the planes were flying . And, probably, no one would require strict evidence if it were not for the desire to refute something. Together with the Greeks, mathematics acquired a new life, in which such problems as the quadrature of a circle, the irrationality of the root of two, and the problem of the trisection of an angle appeared. From this point on, axioms, laws of logic, and theorems were required. Modern mathematics is also interested in what is possible to prove and what is not. The advances were the G√∂del incompleteness theorems, the formalization of logic and the Theory of evidence . I propose a theory and one axiom that will help answer some of the remaining questions and outline the boundaries of our consciousness. In particular, these are questions of completeness, the problem of equality and the axiomatization of our imagination.

Applied sense
We call axiom enumerable if the set of its statements (equalities and inequalities) is enumerable . As follows from the definition, for an enumerable axiomatics, there is an algorithm that can automatically prove theorems and formulate new ones. Moreover, based on our definition of assertions, such an algorithm will be identical to an algorithm that works with a certain algebraic structure. Such an interpretation potentially realizes a long-held dream of ridding mathematicians of inventing evidence.

Conclusion
The results of our research are: the formalization of the internal structure of logical statements, the axiom of imagination, the Theory of objects and four theorems. The latter assert that there are no global equalities in mathematics, the incompleteness of large axiomatics and the derivation of some previously known results and their generalization in a simple way (Continuum hypothesis and G√∂del's Theorem). The description of the structure of logical statements also has an applied meaning, which makes it more convenient to understand the meaning of the sentences, breaking them up into systems of algebraic equalities. Further development implies the search for such logic (topos) in which large axiomatics will be complete. This will provide an opportunity for the unified axiomatization of all mathematics (the theory of everything).
