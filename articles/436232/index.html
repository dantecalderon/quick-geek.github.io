Telepathy strategy


One of the editions of this mechanics

There is a deck of 100 cards with natural numbers from 1 to 100, each one by one. You and the second player draw 3 cards from this deck. The task - one by one, put them on the table in ascending order. But you can not change the information among themselves and show the map until it is placed on the table. That is, you cannot talk, knock on the table, wink and so on - nothing is impossible.

If at the end of the game 6 cards are laid out correctly, both players win. At the moment when the first card goes wrong - both players lose.

Your task is to win in this game. A person you have not seen before is playing with you. He may not even speak languages ‚Äã‚Äãyou know. But he is definitely earthling. And he probably already tried to play this game with other people today. What is your strategy?

First we look at transactions and available information:

  1. There is an act of laying out a card - this is, in fact, the transfer of information that "this is the minimum card of those on my hand, and I consider it minimal in principle."
  2. There is a non-laying out of the map : this means that "I hesitate about the minimal value hypothesis of the map". Unleashing a card is a function of time, that is, a pause between actions also generates a significant event.
  3. There is a visual arrangement of the card on the hand - from where exactly from these three cards does it drag. Most likely, this information is still noise, but it is.

Now we have to think about how to act correctly in this game. Suppose you have cards:

We do not know the opponent's cards.

First approach


The first strategy is to lay out cards in turn with the second player. As it grows. In general, given the distribution, it will make some sense (at least, better than random actions).

But if the second player has a set like 5, 33, 41 in his hands, naturally, this will not work.

Second approximation


We divide the interval from 1 to 100 into 6 parts by the number of cards, we get 16, (6). We believe that each next card should fall into this interval and put it if it lies, and do not put if not. If we have two cards fall in this interval - we put both in a row.

Third approximation


We start counting the expectation of the next card. This means that the longer the interval between our cards in hand, the less likely it is to lay out the next card on our side. We do not lay out our card if the probability indicates that the action must be made by the second player.

All three methods suffer from an obvious disadvantage: they do not guarantee victory.

Fourth approximation


Here we first begin to need the help of a second player. We should get into his head and try to understand how he thinks. Most likely, he is semi-intuitively or consciously comes to something similar - if it is unlikely that the card should be spread, then he will wait. If it seems, then lay out immediately.
For example, if there is a card on the table 45, and he has a card 46 - it is obvious that he will do it almost instantly.
If we put 45, and he has 47, it is also obvious that he will do it instantly, because if we had 46, we would have immediately put it behind 45.
If we have 48, and he did not post 47 at once, it means that you can put 48.
If he sees that we have not posted 48, and he has 49, then he puts it.
That is, further the task turns into the measurement of the function of time between the laying of the cards. But there is an important transition to why this is so.

Go to the interaction of players


When players cannot change information, an interesting part of game theory comes into play. Game theory is generally a strange thing, which assumes that there are only rational people around you. And the earthling who plays with you is also rational.

Game Theory offers the following option: Suppose you could agree before the game. How would you both act optimally?

And the joke is that rational people with the same basic data do not need a dialogue in order to agree. They can accurately predict each other's actions.

That is, the question ‚Äúwhat would we agree on‚Äù is equal in this situation to the question ‚Äúhow would you both act optimally in principle if you knew that the other player would also act optimally?‚Äù

In the ideal case, if our card were 15 units more than the last, and the second player card was 20 units, then we would lay out at the 15th second, and he would at the 20th second.

Which brings us to the model, when you can play blindly and without any players at all. Since we have already decided to count the seconds between the display of cards to give another player a chance to go, then why stop? The model in the finals for any number of players looks like this: everyone silently counts the time and puts the card on the current number of seconds. At the 17th second, you need to lay out a map of 17, if it exists. And so on. To do this, do not even need to see any other actions of the players. And even know how many of them.
